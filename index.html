About Ann

Ann K. Chou (she/her) is a hard-of-hearing, neurodivergent visual artist and access designer, born in Hong Kong and currently active on the unceded lands of the lək̓ʷəŋən and xʷməθkʷəy̓əm peoples—also known as Victoria and Vancouver, BC.

Her practice bridges science and art—from microscope imaging to lantern-building—working with light, shadow, recycled materials, and movement to explore access, ecology, and collective care. Ann celebrates broken objects, spontaneous collaboration, and joyful inclusion through tactile installations, sumi-e brushwork, and mobile lantern sculptures.

She believes things are meant to break—and be beautifully rebuilt.

Her materials often include translucent fabrics, bamboo, garden clippings, mesh, and upcycled plastics, designed for multisensory engagement and adaptability across diverse environments.

Art Meets Service Design

This section will showcase my Master’s thesis and how artistic practice intersects with inclusive service design.

Draw-Aloud Symptom/s: Critical Ethnographic Design of Participant Personal Health Records for Patient Agency

This Master’s thesis explores how people living with chronic or complex conditions express their health journeys through live drawing and spoken reflection. The Draw-Aloud Symptom/s approach invites participants to sketch while narrating their experience—as if explaining to a peer or provider—in safe, familiar environments.

Each participant was provided with a drawing platform—paper, colored pens, pencils, crayons, and watercolor—to support expressive flexibility. For those unable to meet in person (especially during COVID), an at-home remote version was created, offering mailed or drop-off materials and virtual facilitation.

Participants mapped three key “landmarks” of their health journey: the past (onset, memories), the present (current sensations), and the future (hopes and care goals). These moments formed a personal timeline—an alternative to rigid digital forms—designed to reflect story, emotion, and agency.

Sessions began with grounding exercises, with consent reminders throughout. The method honors the emotional vulnerability of illness storytelling. Drawings, metaphors, and reflections were visually coded using the ELITO method—an iterative sticky-note technique capturing values, behaviors, and co-design intentions.

This process offered not just health information, but a visual conversation—where participants co-authored their personal health records as narrative, not just data.

  • 🧩 Method: Critical ethnography + illustrated dialogue (draw-aloud)
  • 🛠️ Tools: Drawing materials + ELITO sticky-note mapping
  • 🌀 Focus: Story, agency, and design justice in health records
